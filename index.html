Early Relations & The Iranian Revolution

Israel and Iran used to be allies
Before the 1979 revolution, Iran and Israel had a surprisingly warm relationship. Iran was one of the first Muslim-majority nations to recognize Israel and even supplied it with oil. Israel had an embassy in Tehran, and the two nations collaborated strategically and economically. This era of alliance is a sharp contrast to the current hostility, showing how geopolitical tides can turn completely. That former closeness now makes today’s enmity all the more intense.

The king fell, and Iran became a religious state
When the Shah was overthrown in 1979, Iran didn’t just change leaders—it changed foundations. The monarchy gave way to a theocracy led by Ayatollah Khomeini, who built the state around strict Shia Islamic principles. Religion wasn’t just a private belief anymore—it became the political and moral backbone of the country. Israel, once an ally, was rebranded as a corrupting, godless force in opposition to Islamic justice. In this new worldview, hostility toward Israel wasn’t just strategic—it was sacred.

Iran's Nuclear Program & The JCPOA

Iran began a nuclear program after its war with Iraq.
After the devastating Iran-Iraq War in the 1980s, Iran sought to rebuild and modernize. One major goal was energy independence, and nuclear power seemed like a promising path. Iran claimed its intentions were peaceful—focused on generating electricity and supporting scientific progress. However, the opaque nature of the program raised suspicions. From the start, the world questioned whether there was more to the effort than just energy.

Iran built machines to make powerful nuclear fuel.
To run a nuclear program, Iran developed centrifuges to enrich uranium, the process needed for fuel. These machines increased the concentration of uranium-235, which is used for both power plants and—at higher levels—nuclear weapons. Over time, Iran installed thousands of centrifuges across the country. Some of its enrichment sites were built deep underground, which made inspections harder. This secrecy only increased global concerns.

A 2015 deal tried to limit Iran’s nuclear program.
The Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action (JCPOA) was signed in 2015 between Iran and six major world powers. Its goal was to prevent Iran from building nuclear weapons by placing strict limits on its program. Iran agreed to reduce uranium enrichment, limit heavy water research, and cap its stockpiles. Restrictions were also placed on centrifuge use and enrichment levels. In return, many nuclear-related sanctions were lifted.

IAEA inspections granted at declared and potential secret sites.
Under the 2015 nuclear deal, Iran allowed the International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA) to monitor both known and suspected nuclear sites. These inspections were some of the most intrusive ever negotiated in a non-proliferation agreement. They helped verify Iran’s compliance and build international trust. Over time, however, tensions would rise as Iran’s cooperation began to wane. The access issue became a key point of contention.

“Snap‑back” UN sanction mechanism in place for ten years.
The deal included a built-in "snap-back" system to automatically reimpose UN sanctions if Iran violated the agreement. This clause was intended to keep pressure on Iran and deter any backsliding. It allowed any of the deal's signatories to trigger the return of sanctions without needing a UN Security Council vote. The mechanism would remain active for ten years. While it offered leverage, its real-world effectiveness remains debated.

Regional Proxy War

Iran armed its friends; now most are ghosts or gone.
For years, Iran funneled weapons, money, and training to proxy groups across the region. From Hezbollah in Lebanon to Hamas in Gaza and militias in Iraq and Yemen, Iran built a vast alliance network. But one by one, these partners have been weakened, killed, or politically sidelined. Once key assets in Iran’s strategy, many now operate in fear or disarray. What was once a sprawling web is now a skeleton of its former strength.

Cyber Warfare: A New Front

Cyber is now a weapon in nuclear-era proxy wars.
Cyberattacks have joined drones, missiles, and sabotage as tools in modern proxy conflicts. Iran and Israel are no longer just fighting with rockets—they’re battling with code, servers, and surveillance. These digital attacks can strike from afar, cause massive disruption, and escalate quickly without crossing borders. In a nuclear-era standoff, cyberwarfare offers plausible deniability and wide reach. It’s no longer a side tactic—it’s central to modern power struggles.

Iran's Geopolitical Strategy

Outlast America—that’s Iran’s long game.
Iran’s foreign policy is built on outlasting rather than outgunning its enemies. Tehran views global power as a cycle and bets on the eventual decline of U.S. dominance. Sanctions, wars, and isolation haven’t changed this view—they’ve refined it. Nuclear negotiations are not signs of surrender but tools to gain time. This strategy, forged during the Iran-Iraq war and refined by years of Western pressure, is driven not by ideology but by hardened survival instincts.

U.S.-Israel Alliance

Israel is the largest recipient of U.S. military aid.
Israel receives more military aid from the United States than any other country in the world. The longstanding assistance reflects both strategic partnership and shared regional interests. This support has allowed Israel to maintain a military edge over its neighbors. Much of the aid flows directly into defense purchases from U.S. companies, reinforcing American defense industries in return. The amount currently stands at $3.8 billion annually.
